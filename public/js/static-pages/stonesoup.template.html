Ideation

Stone Soup evolved out of a desire to bring people together around screens. After reading Sherry Turkles's "Alone Together," I was inspired to create playful interactions that required multiple users in direct proximity to each other. This is the evolution of the project in images.

Because this would be the culminating project for my 3-year MFA, I wrote a rigorous project proposal. You can read the project proposal by clicking the button below.

I developed a digital prototype of the classic playground game four-square on four smartphones. This was a throw-away prototype that I created for purposes of testing the cross-screen mechanic.

With this first prototype, I struck upon the idea of using the proximity and orientation of smartphones to create game mechanics.

From this seed of an idea, I created a multitude of diagrams to represent game ideas. Each idea leveraged screen proximity in a different way.

Several of these ideas were then mocked up with higher fidelity to explore the game concepts in more detail.

With a small team, I set to work creating a game called Eclectic Potatoes. It was about a band of eclectic electric potato musicians. The basic premise of the game was that each player was a different member of the band and they needed to share energy (electricity) to keep playing.

I built and tested a prototype using a shape-matching mechanic across the screen. Players had to find other players in the room with matching shapes then activate the edges of the screen upon finding someone.

The shape-matching game required too many players and was extremely chaotic. I kept the premise of the game (potato) but turned it into a 2-player battle of the bands game. The game was similar to foosball but on tablets with dynamically generated music.

I found myself looking at pop-up books in the children's section of a bookstore one day and thought that it might be interesting to apply the cross-screen technology that I developed to a multi-user storybook. I quickly created this experiment:

The egg demo was a breakthrough! It was way more scalable than the potato game. After 4 months of development, my team and I agreed to put Eclectic Potatoes to rest and focus on a new format of cross-screen storytelling. We invented StoryPops!

The next design challenge was to create various cross-screen interactions for each page. I worked with a screenwriter to adapt the classic folktale of Stone Soup such that each page could feature an interaction. Here are examples of ROUGH storyboards for interactions.

Readers use the handheld device to create wind that blows across the main tablet.
The reader can create a fire to keep the raccoons warm.
By shaking the handheld device, the reader can add stones to the soup. This is a key part of the folktale.
Readers can add salt and pepper to the soup for seasoning.

I prototyped many of the interactions that would be used on different pages. These rough, throwaway prototypes enabled me to test the mechanics and learn from the designs before investing time in the full page illustrations.

After the cross-screen interactions were finalized, we brought the pages to life with illustration, audio, story-text and touch-interactions. Most of these page exports don't include the dynamic content like particles, liquid simulation and other visual effects.
